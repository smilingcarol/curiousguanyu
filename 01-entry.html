How I got here

From language & literature to applied linguistics to human-robot interaction: the thread that connects it all.

My first real encounter with AI came during my PhD. Before that, I studied Chinese language and literature and applied linguistics. I was drawn to how language is used to express meaning, and how that use shifts across social contexts. I became a language teacher, spent years teaching Chinese at universities in the UK, and built a life as someone living and working far from home. My interest in language gradually moved toward its role in human interaction, particularly in intercultural communication, where two people carry different cultural frameworks into the same conversation and still somehow find a way across.

In autumn 2020, my attention turned toward speech and language technologies in computer science. While exploring possible research directions, one concept stopped me: mismatched partners. Coined by Professor Roger Moore, it describes how humans and artificial agents (e.g., robots) are fundamentally mismatched as conversational partners, not in personality or preference, but in interactional capacity. What does interactional capacity actually mean? Why does the mismatch arise? Can it be bridged, and if so, how? What role does language play in causing or repairing it? These questions kept circling in my mind.

The robot I chose to work with was Furhat: a social robot with a face, a voice, and the ability to turn its head, shift expressions, and hold a conversation. My research wasn't about making it smarter. It was about understanding the relationship between how it presents itself and what users expect from that encounter; and how different design choices affect the experience of interacting with it in specific contexts.
