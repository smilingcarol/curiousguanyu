Using AI as a
learning designer

One is chasing. The other is escaping.

How do you use AI at work? Do you think AI will eventually replace your job?

I work as a learning designer. The role centres on transforming existing knowledge into learning materials that are logically coherent, engaging in form, and effective in practice. In concrete terms: structuring content, enriching how it's presented, and making sure it meets the institutional requirements for format and consistency. The tools available include Gemini, a customised ChatGPT, NotebookLM, and a multimedia AI assistant called Robetta.

Using the tools

Over the past several months, the tools I've reached for most are Gemini and NotebookLM. Within Gemini, I've built a range of Gems. They are small, task-specific tools, for things like cross-referencing information during proofreading, checking formatting consistency, simplifying content for different audiences, generating scenario ideas, and more recently, writing HTML to meet specific layout requirements.

The way I approach building these tools follows a consistent pattern: I do the task myself first, map out the constraints and steps, then use that understanding to design and refine something reusable. The exploration phase usually happens alongside Gemini too. I work through the problem with it, then consolidate the key principles, gather feedback from colleagues, and refine again. NotebookLM earns its place by letting me add and update source documents flexibly, pulling from whichever ones are relevant to the task at hand.

When I'm clear on what a task is for, AI tools function like amplifiers or accelerators.

When I'm clear on what a task is for, why it's being done that way, what the standard looks like, and where the judgement calls are, AI tools function like amplifiers or accelerators. The interaction becomes efficient in a way that feels genuinely useful, not just fast.

Beyond the tools

But working with AI at work isn't only about the tools. It's also about navigating how other stakeholders relate to AI. And that, I've found, is considerably more complex. In addition, the adoption of AI, like the adoption of other technologies, may need time to move through recognisable stages. It usually begins with digital literacy, introducing people to what tools exist, what they can do, and how to start using them. From there, the focus tends to shift toward solving specific problems: identifying real pain points in day-to-day work and exploring whether AI can address them. The stage after that could be a process-level integration: connecting workflows so that handoffs become faster and feedback loops become shorter.

This trajectory makes sense. But whether most organisations will travel it smoothly, and how far they'll actually go, remains an open question. This is what I find myself most curious about. Not whether AI will be adopted, but how it might reshape the way organisations actually work: their decision-making processes, their feedback loops, their management structures. Can AI do more than automate individual tasks? Can it help make organisations more responsive, more transparent, more adaptive? And if so, what would need to change in structure, in culture, in the distribution of trust for that to actually happen?

Will AI replace my job?

I don't have answers yet. But I think these are some of the more interesting questions ahead. Which brings me back to the opening question: will AI replace my job as a learning designer?

Well. I think a learning experience isn't just a knowledge structure or a well-designed set of materials. It involves human touch. Students move through learning unevenly, confidence rises and falls, confusion doesn't always come with a clear label, and some of the hardest moments are the ones learners themselves can't fully articulate. These are the places where a human teacher's empathy and professional judgement matter most. Not because AI can't respond, but because recognising what's actually happening requires a kind of understanding that is deeply relational.

The hardest parts of learning have always been human.

There's also the question of personalisation, which I find genuinely exciting to think about. Traditional classroom learning has always been somewhat one-size-fits-all: same materials, same pace, same exercises, feedback from the same teacher at the same time. Technology is changing what's possible. How far can we push personalised learning? Where does AI's role end and the learning designer's begin? How do we design experiences that adapt to individuals without losing the human thread that makes learning meaningful?

I don't have fixed answers. But I'm looking forward to finding out.
